## Eleven Years of Butterfly and Nectar-Producing Plant Community Data from Grassland Sites Managed with Pyric Herbivory 

[![Code DOI](https://img.shields.io/badge/Code-10.5281/zenodo.20329857-blue.svg)](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20329857)

The majority of tallgrass prairie in the American Midwest has been lost, largely due to extensive conversion to agriculture, with negative impacts on prairie-dependent taxa. Pastureland managed with both periodic fire and grazing ("pyric herbivory") to mirror historic disturbance shows promise as a tool to restore grassland species' diversity. The data presented here represent eleven years (2007-18) of butterfly and nectar-producing plant community composition data from 26 sites in the Grand River Grasslands of south-central Iowa (Ringgold Co.) and north-central Missouri (Harrison Co.). All of these sites were managed with one of a few combinations of fire and grazing. An additional anti-tall fescue (_Schedonorus arundinaceus_) herbicide and restoration seedmix treatment was applied between the 2014 and 2015 sampling seasons on a subset of the sites.
